# RAID-Theory
The Self as Real-Time Accumulative Log Data
This paper proposes a physical and information-theoretic model of identity, defining the self as Real-Time Accumulative Log Data (RALD) generated within a high-efficiency parallel computational medium. Under this framework, identity is conceptualized as an append-only, continuously updated log of state transitions embedded in a physical substrate and shaped by ongoing sensorimotor and neural processes.

The paper introduces the concept of local frame binding, suggesting that subjective experience is inherently constrained within a local physical reference frame due to its dependence on medium-bound computation. This provides a non-dualistic account of the apparent spatial persistence of the self without invoking independent non-physical entities.

Furthermore, the model analyzes how the persistence and influence of RALD depend on the properties of its hosting medium. While biological computation terminates at death, informational traces can persist through transmission into other media, including other agents and digital systems.

This framework reframes identity as a medium-dependent informational process rather than a discrete entity, offering a unifying perspective across neuroscience, information theory, and philosophy of mind. It also provides a formal basis for interpreting traditionally metaphysical concepts (e.g., the “soul”) as emergent descriptions of medium-bound informational continuity.
